Canverse

Music-making simplified.

The Problem

Creating digital music today is far too complicated. The common software tools offered today are made with multiple confusing widgets, dials and options that can make creating music difficult for even an in-depth user.

While creating original music is an artistic, creative and free flowing process, most modern software limits this by enforcing a rigid left to right timeline approach. This is not well-suited for playing free form instruments such as synthesizers, and also disrupts the creation of a rhythmic beat, forcing the user to unnaturally reason about how to space out notes when they would rather just tap their beat out.

Our goal is to break the uniform structure of most musical software tools offered today and provide the freedom to make creating digital music natural and fun.

The Solution

The design of Canverse was very important to us. We knew that to successfully solve the problems found with most music-making software we had to focus not just on creating a minimalistic interface, but also one powerful enough to make great music.

The traditional timeline and cluttered interfaces are gone. Canverse presents in their place a simple canvas to play notes on, your gateway to a session full of fun and experimentation. We complement this with a display of both your session's history and the various loops repeating in the background, so you can keep track of what's being played.

Synths

Creating varied melodies necessitates the use of multiple synths to combine different sounds. To minimize the amount of interface clutter, Canverse controls changing synths using the up and down arrow keys. This allows you to quickly and efficiently flip through over 15 available instruments, each with unique names to help you identify them.

In most modern-day software, controlling synth parameters requires digging around through piles of dials and sliders. Rather than bog you down with this, we isolated two essential parameters and incorporated them right into the canvas. As you slide your mouse horizontally along the canvas, the frequency of the synth being played increases, while dragging the cursor vertically changes the amplitude. The canvas responds to your movements, showing darker to lighter colors as the frequency increases and fading in and out as the amplitude fluctuates.

Looping

A crucial component of any electronic musician's repertoire is the ability to layer sound after sound effortlessly and seamlessly, taking simple noises and combining them into something unexpected and fantastic. In Canverse, simply play a note and press the spacebar to immediately begin looping it back. Multiple notes can be looped by selecting them in the timeline with your mouse before pressing space.

This creates a node on the rightmost column of the window flashing to the rhythm of the loop. This node controls your loop, allowing you to adjust its volume by dragging the node up or down and to toggle it on and off by double-clicking. This simple abstraction provides the flexibility and power necessary to progress your song the way you want.

Drums

Drums are the driving force behind every electronic masterpiece. Canverse recognizes that your beats and synths exist cohesively and keeps them grouped together rather than force you to awkwardly switch to a separate widget. A single click of your right mouse button on the canvas turns a drum loop on and off. Each row controls a different loop, and flashes a different color as the loop plays. In response to the beat, the canvas pulses, making your software come alive with your music.

Implementing this eliminated the need to introduce any new dials or toggles on the interface to control drums and allows the user to add both synths and drum loops without lifting their hands off the mouse.

Finally, to allow you to customize the tempo of your track as much as possible, you can increase and decrease the speed of the drum loop by pressing the left and right arrow keys on the keyboard. As the tempo increases or decreases so does the amount the of shaking on the grid to provide a visual confirmation of how fast or slow the tempo is playing.

Implementation

Innovation requires experimentation. For this reason we chose to use the Overtone audio environment, which is designed to facilitate the exploration of new musical ideas with tools that allow for fine grained synthesis and sampling. This allowed for fast prototyping of mock-ups which we heavily benefited from during the early stages of development, where fine-tuning audio parameters could be done without having a fully-functioning graphical application.

One additional benefit to using Overtone is it combines the powerful SuperCollider audio engine with the Clojure programming language which provides the interactivity and efficiency needed for a project such as Canverse. This enabled us to receive instant feedback from changes to the application throughout our development cycle.

After finalizing the audio and programming environment for this project, we decided to use Quil, a Clojure wrapper around the Processing environment, which is geared towards visual design and greatly facilitates the creation of graphical interfaces.

Synths

At the core of our application we have synths, providing the audio feedback needed to create the musical soundscapes which our project aims to produce. These synths are the core functionality that is provided by Overtone, which exposes synth parameters that can be dynamically updated. In Canverse, just like in Overtone, synths are encapsulated by node objects which contain information about the synth to be played and the specific parameters to be applied, including the frequency and amplitude. Furthermore, Overtone provided the functionality to bring into the application sampled audio data which we were able to leverage in order to present the user with a large variety of synths.

Timeline

To implement the timeline functionality we keep track of the currently playing nodes and record their frequency and amplitude after each update. This recorded information is then used to control how the timeline will be drawn; frequency alters the vertical position and color of the timeline while amplitude alters the opacity.

Looping

When creating a loop, we read the synth and the frequency and amp values off of each node selected. We use this to create another node whose frequency and amplitude is controlled to match up with the values that should play at any given point, muting it during periods of silence. When the loop finishes, its current playback time is reset back to zero, making it play again.

Drums

There were two complications with implementing drums. One was keeping separate drum loops from going off beat. Our solution was to keep an internal metronome, provided by Overtone, which we used to keep all drum loops in time. This allowed us to layer basic drum loops on top of each other in order create more elaborate drum sequences. Additionally, using a metronome allowed us to expose tempo to the user.

The second complication was with turning drum loops off. When a drum loop is initially scheduled, it returns a job ID that can be used to turn the loop off on request. However, since each drum loop we implemented was recursive and would schedule itself again after four beats had passed, the job ID of the loop in progress being kept would become out of date after the first run, preventing us from cancelling it. This necessitated keeping a hash mapping drum loop names to current job IDs that would be updated each time a drum loop was run, so we could keep track of the job IDs from the recursive calls.
